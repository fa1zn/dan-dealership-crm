Executive summary

Dan is an internal tool that a territory sales rep opens every working day to sell Pam into franchise car dealerships. Pam is the product being sold: an AI that answers the phone for a business, so a dealership stops leaking the service and sales calls it is already paying to generate. Dan is the thing that decides which dealership is worth calling today, tells the rep who to ask for and why, runs the outreach, and keeps every touch on one record so nothing is dropped.

The strategic thesis is simple. Pam's growth is gated by field distribution, and the reps are the distribution. Every hour a rep spends fighting a spreadsheet, guessing who to call, or hunting for a name is an hour not spent in a conversation that could book a demo. So arming the rep is leverage on Pam's entire go-to-market, and the productivity gain compounds across the whole field organization at close to zero marginal cost.

Dan is three things stacked together, and none works alone. It is a system of record: a cleaned, provenance-tracked census of roughly 34,000 franchise new-car rooftops across the United States and Canada. It is an intelligence layer on top of that record: it answers the rep's real morning question, who do I call, why, and what do I say, and it puts a named decision-maker with a real direct dial in front of the rep before the call. And it is an orchestrated outreach motion: a compliance-first call, then text, then gift cadence that opens with a genuine inquiry ("are you hiring for any roles?") rather than a cold pitch, so it is both lawful and effective, and that a rep can run by hand or let Dan run on autopilot, with every touch landing on one shared timeline.

Two design commitments distinguish Dan from any list or CRM the reps have been handed before.

The first is provenance. Every value Dan shows carries a defensible source. Hover a phone number, an address, a brand, and Dan tells you where it came from and when. Values it inferred rather than fetched are labeled as inferred, in plain words, on every record. A rep can flip the entire application between an Enriched view and the Raw scraped source with a single, persisted toggle. Trust comes from "here's the source," not "trust us," and that stance is threaded through the whole product rather than bolted on.

The second is honesty as a design value. Blank beats wrong. Validation says "not yet verified" rather than a false "invalid." There is no gamified cringe, no internal marketing to the rep, no manufactured urgency on an empty day. The product is built for a non-technical road warrior who has been burned by every CRM he has ever seen, and it earns his trust by refusing to overstate itself.

This document describes Dan as it actually exists, from four angles. Part I is the customer and the user: who Pam is, who the rep is, how a dealership is organized and how it buys, and why the compliance-first inquiry motion is the right wedge. Part II walks the product screen by screen, exactly as it renders today. Part III is the technology, written for an engineering leader who has to trust an autonomous outbound motion before signing off on it, with every load-bearing claim tied to real code. Part IV is the feel and the user experience, the honest state of what works and what is still rough, and a sequenced roadmap for how Dan reaches its next level, from finishing the data foundation to proactive location-aware intelligence to a supervised autonomous BDR.

A note on candor, because it is the point. The sections that follow are specific about what is shipped and working (real Google-verified data, real direct dials, real HubSpot reconciliation, a working call-to-text-to-gift motion with real safety rails) and equally specific about what is not yet hardened (the franchise gate and dedup are not finished at full scale, contact coverage is still thin, the consent gate is coarser than it will be, OEM coverage depends on a proxy, and there is no login yet). That candor is not a hedge. It is the same discipline that makes the product trustworthy: a tool that tells you where its data came from, and where it is still weak, is a tool you can actually build on.

Part I

Who this is for, the wedge, and go-to-market

Who this is for: understanding the customer and the user

Before describing what Dan does, it helps to be precise about who Dan is for. There are three distinct parties, and they are easy to blur together. Pam is the company that pays for the outcome. The territory rep is the person who lives inside Dan every working day. The dealership is the target the rep is trying to reach and win. Each has a different definition of success, a different tolerance for friction, and a different relationship to the truth. Dan works only if it serves all three at once, and it fails the moment it optimizes for one at the expense of the others. So we draw each layer carefully.

Pam, the company

Pam sells an AI voice agent that answers the phone for a business. For a car dealership, that means when a customer calls the service department at 7:40 in the morning, or the sales floor at 9:15 at night, or during the lunch hour when the receptionist is covering three lines at once, Pam picks up. Pam answers in natural speech, understands what the caller wants, and does the work: it books a service appointment against the live scheduling system, it captures a sales lead and routes it, it answers the routine questions that eat a receptionist's day (hours, directions, "is my car ready," "do you have this trim in stock"), and it does all of this around the clock, in more than one language, without putting anyone on hold. The pitch is not "a better answering machine." It is that the dealership stops leaking the calls it is already paying to generate.

That framing matters because it is grounded in a number every dealer already feels. Dealerships spend heavily on advertising, and a large share of that spend converts into inbound phone calls. Industry studies of dealership call handling have long shown that a meaningful fraction of inbound calls go unanswered, get sent to voicemail, or are mishandled at the point of contact. A missed sales call is not a small thing. A single sold vehicle carries front and back gross that dwarfs the monthly cost of the software. So Pam's value proposition is unusually clean for a business-software product: it plugs a hole the customer can already see, in a system the customer already trusts to be the front door to their revenue.

Pam's problem is not the product. It is distribution. There are roughly 34,000 franchise new-car rooftops across the United States and Canada, and they do not buy software the way a tech company buys software. They are local, relationship-driven, skeptical of vendors, and protective of the phone because the phone is where deals happen. You do not win these accounts with a self-serve signup page and a webinar. You win them with a person who shows up, earns a few minutes of a busy manager's attention, and makes the case in terms the manager cares about. That is why Pam needs an army of territory reps. The reps are the distribution. They are the difference between a good product that a few hundred dealers have heard of and a category-defining product that becomes the default way dealerships answer their phones.

Once you accept that the reps are the distribution, the leverage becomes obvious. Every hour a rep spends fighting a spreadsheet, guessing which dealership to call, or hunting for the right person's name is an hour not spent in a conversation that could book a demo. Rep productivity is not a soft metric here. It is the multiplier on Pam's entire go-to-market. If Dan makes each rep meaningfully more effective (more of the right conversations, fewer wasted dials, cleaner follow-through), that improvement compounds across the whole field organization. Arming the reps with better tooling is not a nice-to-have on the side of the real business. For a company whose growth is gated by field distribution, it is one of the highest-leverage investments available. This is the case Dan is built to make good on.

The territory sales rep, the daily user

The person who actually opens Dan every morning is a territory sales rep, and it is worth describing them without romance. They are a road warrior. Their office is their car. Their territory is a geographic patch (a metro, a cluster of counties, a stretch of a state) containing some number of dealerships they are responsible for. They are measured on two things that roll up into one: meetings booked and deals closed. Everything else is noise to them, and they are right to treat it that way, because that is how they are paid and how they keep their job.

They are, importantly, not technical. They did not come from software. Many of them came from car sales, from field sales in other industries, from the kind of work where charm, persistence, and knowing your patch matter more than any tool. They are extremely good at the human part of the job and have very little patience for the software part. This is the single most important fact about the user, and it shapes every design decision in Dan.

Here is a rep's day, roughly. They wake up somewhere in their territory, maybe a hotel, maybe home if they are lucky. They need to know, before they have finished their coffee, who they are going to talk to today and why. They will spend the day some combination of on the phone and physically driving between rooftops, walking into service drives and showrooms, asking for a manager, getting turned away, getting a name, occasionally getting five real minutes with a decision-maker. Between stops they are in the car doing follow-up: a text to someone they met last week, a note to themselves about a conversation, a reminder to send something. In the evening they are supposed to update the CRM, and this is the part they hate.

They hate it for a specific reason, and it is not laziness. CRMs, as reps experience them, are data-entry machines built for managers to watch reps, not tools built for reps to sell. The rep is asked to type in what they already know, into fields that do not match how the work actually happened, for the benefit of a dashboard they will never look at. Every minute spent feeding the CRM is a minute stolen from selling, and the CRM gives them nothing back. So they resist it, they do it badly and late, and the data rots, which makes the CRM even less useful, which makes them resist it more. This is a doom loop, and every rep-facing tool that has ever failed has failed inside it.

The rep's actual mental model is simple, and Dan is built to match it exactly. Three verbs: find, work, track. Find the dealership or the person worth talking to today. Work the account: make the call, send the text, log what happened, move it forward. Track where everything stands so nothing falls through and so the rep knows what to do next without having to reconstruct it from memory. That is the whole job. A rep does not think in terms of "pipeline stages" or "activity logging" or "data hygiene." They think, "Who do I call, why, and what do I say." A tool that answers those three questions instantly, and asks for as little typing as possible in return, is a tool a rep will actually use. A tool that inverts that (that makes the rep do work to feed the tool before the tool gives anything back) is a tool the rep will abandon, no matter how good it looks in a demo. Dan is designed around this asymmetry: give first, ask second, and never make the rep type something the system could have known.

The dealership, the end target

The target is a franchise new-car dealership, what the industry calls a rooftop. This is a specific and well-structured world, and understanding its org chart is essential because Dan's entire outreach motion depends on reaching the right human inside it.

At the top of a single rooftop is the dealer principal or owner, sometimes running a single store, increasingly part of a dealer group that owns many. Below the principal is the general manager (GM), who runs the store as a business and is usually the person whose signature or blessing a deal of this size needs. Under the GM sits the general sales manager (GSM), who owns the sales floor and its numbers. Then there are the department heads and specialists who touch the phone directly: the BDC (business development center) manager, who runs the team whose entire job is handling inbound and outbound calls and setting appointments, and the internet or digital manager, who owns online leads and increasingly the phone experience that connects to them. Service has its own management chain, and the service drive is often where the phone pain is most acute, because service calls are high-volume, repetitive, and relentless.

The reason this org chart matters is that Pam's product lands differently with each of these people. The BDC manager feels the missed-call pain most directly and can be a champion, but rarely holds the budget. The GM holds the budget and cares about gross and about anything that touches the customer experience, but is hard to reach and guards their time. The internet manager sits at the intersection and is often the most receptive to a technology pitch. A rep who knows which door to knock on, and who is actually behind it, has an enormous advantage over a rep working from a generic "ask for the manager" script. Dan's job is to put the right name and the right role in front of the rep before the call, so the rep is not burning their opening seconds figuring out who they are even talking to.

How dealerships buy is its own thing. They are skeptical of vendors by disposition, because they are pitched constantly, by everyone, on everything. They buy on trust and on proof, usually through a relationship that has been warmed over multiple touches, and often only after a peer at another store has vouched for the thing. The sales cycle is a series of small human wins (getting a name, getting a callback, getting a demo, getting a trial) rather than a single transaction. This is exactly the kind of motion a well-armed rep wins and a spreadsheet loses.

And then there is compliance, which is not a footnote. Territory reps are selling business-to-business, but the way they reach dealerships is still governed by real law, and the relevant reality here is the framework around unsolicited solicitation and telephone contact (in the United States, the TCPA and related rules; there are analogous regimes in Canada). You cannot simply cold-pitch a business's published lines with a sales solicitation the way the old boiler-room playbook did, especially at scale and especially with any automated assistance. The legal and reputational exposure is real, and it is exactly the kind of exposure a company selling a phone-answering product cannot afford to create for itself. This constraint is not an obstacle Dan works around. It is a fact Dan is built to respect, and, as the next section argues, respecting it turns out to be a competitive advantage rather than a tax.

The core insight and the wedge

The core insight behind Dan is that the rep does not need more leads. They are drowning in a list of 34,000 rooftops already. What the rep needs is the opposite of more: they need a system that decides, for them, who is worth talking to today, hands them everything they need to have that conversation, and then carries the conversation forward through the exact sequence of touches that is both legal and effective. Dan is not a lead list. It is a system of record, plus intelligence layered on top of it, plus an orchestrated outreach motion that runs on rails the rep can trust. Those three things together are the right shape for this problem, and none of the three works alone.

The system of record is the foundation. A rep cannot work an account they cannot see, and they cannot trust intelligence sitting on top of data they know is stale. Dan holds a clean, current picture of every rooftop in the rep's territory: the dealership, its franchise, its people, its history of contact, and where each account stands right now. This is the "track" verb made real, and crucially it is a record the rep does not have to hand-build, because Dan builds and maintains it for them.

The intelligence is what turns a record into a recommendation. Given the record, Dan answers the rep's real morning question (who do I call, why, and what do I say) instead of dumping a sortable table on them and wishing them luck. It surfaces the accounts where a touch is due, where a person has just been identified, where a prior conversation left an opening. It puts the right name, role, and direct line in front of the rep before the call. This is the difference between a tool that stores information and a tool that produces decisions.

The orchestrated outreach motion is where the insight gets sharp, because it is a specific sequence, and the sequence is designed around the compliance reality rather than in spite of it. The motion is an inquiry, not a pitch. The rep calls the dealership and asks a genuine, permitted question: are you hiring for any roles? Dealerships are almost always hiring (sales turnover is famously high), so this is a real question with a real answer, and it is not a solicitation. The person who picks up will often, helpfully, point the rep toward the person who handles hiring or the manager who runs that part of the store. Now the rep has something they did not have before: a name, a role, and a warm reason they were given that name. The next touch is a text to that person, human and specific, referencing the conversation. And then, where it fits, a small gift, a genuine gesture that opens the door to a real relationship. Call, then text, then gift. Each step earns the right to the next.

It is worth being exact about why this is legal where spray-and-pray cold calling is not, and why it is more effective, not just safer. The inquiry motion is legal because the opening contact is not a sales solicitation. It is a legitimate business question that the dealership is happy to answer, and it produces a person who has, in effect, been introduced to the rep by the dealership itself. Every subsequent touch is directed at a specific individual in the context of that introduction, which is a completely different legal and human posture than blasting a published line with an automated pitch. The rep never has to pretend, never has to hide the ball, and never has to do the thing that creates TCPA-style exposure. The compliance-first design is not a compromise that makes the motion weaker. It is the thing that makes the motion work.

Contrast this with the default playbook, spray-and-pray cold calling. The old motion is: get a list, dial every number, deliver a pitch, and grind through rejection on volume. It is legally fraught at scale, it is miserable for the rep, it is annoying to the dealership, and it is inefficient because it treats every rooftop identically and burns the rep's scarcest resource (their time and their emotional energy) on conversations that were never going to go anywhere. The inquiry motion inverts every one of these. It opens with a question the dealership wants to answer instead of a pitch they want to escape. It produces a named human instead of a dead-end dial. It gives the rep a reason to follow up that the dealership handed them. And it respects the compliance line by construction, so neither the rep nor Pam is carrying risk they cannot see. This is the wedge: not a better list, but a better motion, one that is legal, humane, and effective at the same time, delivered to the rep on rails so they do not have to invent it call by call.

This is real, not a slide. The call-to-text-to-gift motion exists and runs today. The system of record behind it is populated with real, verified data (discussed next), including real direct lines to the managers the rep needs. The intelligence that decides who to call is working against that record now. Where the product is still growing (deeper automation of the sequence, tighter loops back into the record, more of the follow-through handled for the rep) we will say so plainly in the sections that follow. But the core shape is not aspirational. The wedge is in the field.

Go-to-market and why this wins

Dan's go-to-market case rests on a single compounding claim: Dan makes each Pam rep more productive, that productivity is the multiplier on Pam's whole distribution, and the thing Dan is built on (a clean, provenance-tracked census of North American franchise dealerships) is a durable advantage that gets harder to copy over time rather than easier. Let us take those in order.

How Dan compounds rep productivity

The rep's day, described earlier, has a lot of dead weight in it: deciding who to call, finding the right person, reconstructing where an account stands, fighting the CRM at night. Every one of those is time not spent in a conversation. Dan removes them, and the removals stack. When the rep opens Dan and is handed the day's accounts already prioritized, they skip the deciding. When the right name and direct line are already in front of them, they skip the hunting. When the record is current because Dan maintained it, they skip the reconstructing. When logging a call is a tap instead of a form, they skip the nightly data-entry misery, which means the record actually stays current, which means tomorrow's prioritization is good, which means the rep trusts it, which means they use it. That last loop is the important one. Most rep tools die in the doom loop where bad data makes the tool useless and a useless tool gets bad data. Dan is designed to spin the loop the other way: give the rep something valuable before asking for anything, so the record stays alive, so the value keeps compounding.

Multiply a modestly more productive rep across a field organization and the arithmetic is straightforward. If Dan turns even a slice of each rep's wasted hours into conversations, and turns a slice of those conversations into booked meetings, that improvement lands on top of Pam's existing spend on those reps at close to zero marginal cost. This is why arming the reps is leverage on the whole GTM rather than a line item. The reps are the distribution channel, and Dan is a productivity multiplier applied to the entire channel at once.

The data moat

Underneath all of this is an asset that is genuinely hard to build and genuinely hard to copy: a cleaned, provenance-tracked census of roughly 34,000 franchise new-car rooftops across the United States and Canada. Anyone can scrape a list of dealership names. Almost no one can produce a list where every rooftop is verified, deduplicated, correctly attributed to its franchise and group, enriched with the specific people a rep needs to reach, and (this is the part that matters) carrying a record of where each field came from.

Provenance is the moat inside the moat. In this product, every field is sourced. The dealership record is Google-verified for the things Google can verify (the rooftop exists, its location, its public contact points). The people and their direct lines come from enrichment against a real contact-data provider, so that when Dan puts a GM's direct dial in front of a rep, that number has a known origin rather than being a guess. The reconciliation against the CRM of record (HubSpot) keeps Dan's picture and Pam's official picture in agreement, so the rep is never working from a shadow copy that has drifted from the truth. This is shipped, working infrastructure: real Google-verified dealership data, real direct dials for the managers reps need, real reconciliation against the live CRM.

The reason "raw plus enriched in one product, every field sourced" is a durable trust advantage, and not just a nice feature, is that it solves the problem that kills every data product eventually: the user stops believing it. A rep who gets one wrong number, one dead account, one name of someone who left two years ago, starts discounting the whole tool, and once trust is gone it does not come back. Provenance is how Dan earns and keeps that trust. When a rep can see that a number came from a real enrichment source, that a dealership was verified against Google, that the record agrees with HubSpot, they extend the tool credit, and they keep using it even when a field is occasionally imperfect, because they can see the machinery and trust its intentions. A competitor can copy the list. They cannot easily copy the accumulated provenance, the reconciliation plumbing, and the trust that compounds from both. That is what makes this a moat rather than a feature.

We should be honest about the boundary of the claim. The census is real and the enrichment is real, and coverage and freshness are things any data asset has to keep earning rather than declare finished. The advantage is not that Dan's data is perfect on any given day. It is that Dan's data is sourced, so its quality is legible and improvable, which is a fundamentally stronger position than a black-box list that is either trusted blindly or not at all.

The land-and-expand path

The entry point is narrow and sharp on purpose: arm one rep, or one small field team, with Dan, and let the productivity difference show up in their numbers. This is a land motion that does not require a big up-front commitment or a platform decision from Pam's leadership. It requires letting the tool prove itself where the work actually happens, in a territory, against real accounts, with a real rep who will tell you honestly whether it made their day better.

From that landing, expansion is natural because the value grows with adoption. More reps on Dan means more of the field maintaining the record through their daily work, which makes the record better for everyone, which makes the intelligence sharper, which makes each additional rep more productive than the last. The data moat and the productivity multiplier reinforce each other as the footprint grows. And because Dan is built to augment the rep and to reconcile with the systems Pam already runs on rather than replace them, expansion does not require ripping anything out. It layers on. The path is: land with a rep who feels the lift, expand across the team as the record and the intelligence compound, and grow into the full field organization as the thing Pam's distribution runs on. That is the shape of a durable go-to-market, and it is grounded in a product that works in the field today rather than a roadmap that hopes to.

Part II

The product, screen by screen

The product

Dan is the tool a territory rep opens to sell Pam into franchise car dealerships. Pam is the product being sold: an AI that answers the phone for a business. Dan is the thing that finds the dealerships worth calling, tells the rep who to ask for and why, runs the outreach, and keeps every touch on one record so nothing gets dropped. It is a working Next.js application, not a deck. What follows describes it as it exists.

The first thing to understand about Dan is its point of view. It is not a database with a sales skin bolted on, and it is not a dialer with a spreadsheet attached. It is a rep's copilot that happens to sit on top of a very large book of business. Everything in the interface is bent toward one question a rep asks all day: who do I call next, and what do I say? When the product is unsure, it says so plainly. When it inferred something rather than fetched it, it labels that. That restraint is the personality of the whole thing.

The sidebar makes the identity literal. The logo is a single serif Dan, and directly under it, in muted text: Pam's sales guy. At the very bottom of the rail: Book of business — North American franchise rooftops. Dan is a person on your team. Pam is the product he sells. The rooftops are the territory. That framing holds all the way down.

The shape: three verbs

The navigation is deliberately short. Above a thin Setup divider, there are four primary destinations, and they map to a rep's actual day rather than to a data model:

  • Today — what to do right now.
  • Prospect — put Dan on a market and let him work it.
  • Territory — where am I, and who's near me.
  • Book — the whole system of record.

Below the divider, Connections and Setup (Integrations) hold the plumbing a rep configures once and forgets.

If you collapse that into a mental model, it is three verbs: Find work, Work it, Track it. Prospect and Territory are how you find the next thing to do, one by market, one by geography. The rooftop record and the sales motion are how you work it. Today and Book are how you track what is happening, at two zoom levels: Today is the single-screen "what needs me," Book is the full ledger. Connections and Integrations are neither; they are the one-time wiring that makes the rest run.

The reason this matters is that a rep never has to translate. There is no "leads" tab that is secretly a filtered query, no "campaigns" that are really cron jobs. The words on the nav are the words a rep already uses. On a phone the same five destinations appear as a native-feeling bottom tab bar (Today, Prospect, Territory, Book, Setup), because reps use this standing in a dealership parking lot as often as at a desk.

One control lives in the top bar on every screen and deserves its own mention up front, because it changes what all the other screens show: the Enriched ⇄ Raw toggle. It is covered in full under Book and under provenance, but keep it in mind as you read, it is the seam where Dan's honesty about its own data becomes a feature you can hold in your hand.

There is also a small, quiet status cluster in the top bar. It reads the rep's actual configuration and states it in plain words: Practice mode or Calling for real, an optional Test mode chip when calls are being routed to a test number, and Dan's working / Dan's paused for whether autopilot is on. When manual calling is set, it even says You tap to call. None of this is decorative. A rep should never be unsure whether a real dealer is about to get a real phone call, and the bar answers that at a glance.

Screen by screen

Today — the copilot

Today is the home screen and the thesis of the product in miniature. Its job is to answer "what do I do right now" before the rep has finished their coffee, and to do it without turning the job into a game.

The screen leads with the next action, not a statistic. At the top, under a small flame and the label Do this next, sits a single prominent card: one dealership, its brand and city, a Hot pill, a one-line reason it is worth a call right now, and a full-width Call now button that dials straight from the phone. Below it, under Then these, the rest of the queue appears as compact rows, enough to decide, not a second full card each. Every row has its own reason line and its own call button.

The reason line is the part that makes this more than a list. It is generated per lead and it is specific. The logic combines what Pam last heard on the account with how stale it has gotten, on the principle that a hot lead you haven't touched in days is the one about to cool. So a rep sees things like "replied today, strike while it's warm" or "engaged, 4d since last touch, follow up before it cools," not "in your territory." The queue is ordered most-overdue first, so the top card is a defensible "do this next" rather than a recency coin flip.

Honesty note, and it is in the code as a comment: that most-overdue ordering is explicitly a hypothesis. The intent is to validate it against the real ride-along event stream before trusting it as the permanent order. That is the right posture, and it is worth saying in the doc rather than papering over: the ranking is good enough to ship and not yet proven to be optimal.

Below the queue is Your month, the pipeline goal card. This is where a lesser product would gamify. Dan does not. The card shows deals closed against goal (e.g. "3 / 8"), dollars in pipeline, a single thin progress bar, and a quiet footer breaking out open / booked / closed counts. There are no streaks, no confetti, no leaderboard. It reads like something a professional would tolerate seeing every morning for a year. The framing, "Goal 8 · $X/deal," treats the rep as an adult who is here to make money, not a user to be nudged.

At the bottom, What Dan's been doing is a live activity feed: calls placed, texts sent, gifts sent, sequence movements, status changes, each with an icon and a one-line summary, each linking to the dealer it happened on. This is the "my AI teammate has been busy while I slept" surface. It is what makes the autopilot legible instead of spooky.

Empty states are handled with the same calm voice. When there is genuinely nothing that needs the rep, the screen does not invent urgency. It says You're all caught up, and then, depending on state, either "Dan's working N dealers. The moment one bites, they'll show up right here" or, if nothing is running at all, "Head to Prospect, point Dan at a market, and he'll start the calls." That second sentence is doing quiet onboarding, it tells a brand-new rep exactly where to go next.

Today is one of the strongest screens in the product. It has a clear job, it does that job, and it resists the temptation to be busier than it needs to be.

Book / Accounts — the system of record

Book is the ledger: the full set of North American franchise rooftops, roughly 34,000 of them, sitting behind a fast, filterable table. Where Today is one screen of "what needs me," Book is "everything, on demand." The header states the honest count of what the current filters return, "N dealers in your territory," rather than a marketing number.

Two views share the header, switched by a List ⇄ Board tab pair. List is the default: a dense table with sortable columns for Dealership, Status, OEM, Tier, City, State, Country, Territory, plus two compact health dots for Web and Tel. Two features on this screen carry real weight.

Export CSV. A single outlined button in the header. It respects the current filters, it hands off the exact same query string the table is showing, so "export what I'm looking at" does what a rep expects. This is the escape hatch that makes reps trust a system of record: they can always get their working list out.

The Enriched ⇄ Raw toggle. This is the product-wide data-layer switch, and Book is where its meaning is clearest. The toggle lives in the top bar as a small pill with Enriched (a sparkle) and Raw (a database icon). CSS then hides the opposite layer everywhere: a name that Dan normalized shows the cleaned version in Enriched and the original scraped string in Raw; a phone number Google filled in appears in Enriched and vanishes in Raw. The philosophy is one product, both layers, flip anytime. A skeptical buyer or a careful rep can always drop to Raw and see exactly what was scraped, with nothing polished on top.

That the toggle is global and persisted is the right call and the harder one to build. It means "trust but verify" is always one click away from anywhere in the app, which is precisely the posture a data product should want a skeptic to be in.

The rooftop record — the briefing

This is the most information-dense screen in the product, and it is organized as a briefing: the things you need before you dial are at the top, the reference material is below a "More on this dealer" divider. A rep should be able to open this cold and be ready to call in ten seconds.

The header carries the dealership name (itself provenance-wrapped, so hovering shows the source; in Raw view it swaps to the original scraped name), the CRM status badge, a tier badge, and, when present, a trust tier badge ("gold · 2 sources," "platinum · 3 sources") that counts how many independent sources confirmed this rooftop is real. Directly below is the Why call card, the single most important block on the page. Your turn, give them a call."; if Dan reached out with no reply, "Dan reached out. No reply yet, worth a personal call."; if it's fresh, a specific angle or "Fresh lead, start with a call." Every value on the strip is marked as pulled live from Google Places. There is a genuinely important piece of honesty engineered into this card. Roughly 80% of rooftops have no phone number on file from the raw sources. Google fills that gap. Dan tracks that distinction rather than hiding it: when the number came from Google, the Call button reads "Call (number) · found on Google," and that button only shows in Enriched view. In Raw, a rep sees the store had no phone on file. The product refuses to launder a Google-sourced fact into a "we had this all along" fact.

Next is Outreach, the per-rooftop motion card. In short: a horizontal call → text → treat stepper, a temperature pill, a "next action" line, an armed action button, and a compliance line that reads "Dan always says he's an AI and never sells on the first call. Note the labeling discipline: brand and phone-on-file are sourced (OEM locator, OpenStreetMap record), while dealer group, group size, and territory are wrapped in an "Estimated by Dan, inferred from other fields, not fetched from a source" tooltip. Inferred and sourced never wear the same clothes.

The nuance here is careful: "not yet verified" is neutral and grey, never alarming, and a branded store never shows a red "Invalid" on its brand, it is either confirmed or simply not-yet-confirmed. The card ends with the list of sources that contributed to this rooftop.

Log a touch is the manual logging strip, and it is one of the quiet stars of the record. This is the seam between the human rep and the AI teammate: they write to the same timeline. This is where Log-a-touch and the autopilot both land.

Its purpose is stated in the integration itself, so a rep can see what's already being worked and never double-prospect a dealer a colleague owns. Dan reads HubSpot; it never writes back.

Rounding out the right rail: Why Pam fits (angle sentences plus "spotted on their site" tech-stack evidence, each with the exact matched string in its tooltip), Contacts, Tech stack, Signals (rating, hours, email pattern, socials from schema.org), and an embedded Location map. The record is the densest screen and, honestly, the one most at risk of overwhelming a first-time rep. The information architecture does the work of keeping it from being a wall.

Territory — location-aware intelligence

Territory answers a question the table cannot: I'm physically here today, who should I see while I'm in the area? It is built for the rep in the field, not at the desk.

The screen opens with a plain prompt, "Where are you today?", and a city selector seeded with the rep's top markets. Pick a city, and Dan reorganizes everything around it. A one-line summary states the shape of the area honestly: "N dealers in {city} · X in your pipeline · Y not yet contacted."

Below is a live map. Dealers are plotted as numbered pins in the route order Dan already computed, split visually between "in your pipeline" and "not yet contacted." The map drops a live "you are here" dot from the rep's device location, so it moves as they drive. It is built on Leaflet and OpenStreetMap tiles. If the rep has connected a calendar, that day's appointments with locations are plotted too, and a Calendar panel shows the day's schedule.

Under the map, two lists make the trip concrete. "In your pipeline · check in while you're here" lists the numbered stops the rep is already working, each showing its status. "Not yet contacted · worth a stop" lists the open dealers in the area (capped at 20 with a "+N more" line). Both lists use the same numbering as the map pins, so "stop 3 on the map" is "row 3 in the list." Every row links straight into that dealer's record.

The design intent is stated right on the page: "Dan clusters your dealers by area so you can see everyone in one trip, in an efficient order." This is the "don't drive past three prospects to see one" feature. Concretely, the Denver case: a rep in Denver picks it from the selector and gets one map with numbered stops, their three in-pipeline accounts to check in on, plus the highest-value untouched stores nearby, ordered so the drive makes sense, instead of a spreadsheet they'd have to hand-plan.

Territory is a strong idea, cleanly executed for the core loop. The honest edges: the route order is a clustering heuristic, not a solved travelling-salesman route with live traffic, and the "you are here" dot and calendar plotting depend on device permissions and a connected calendar respectively. For the job it is doing, "who's near me, in what rough order," it is right-sized.

The value of this screen is that it makes an autopilot legible. The sales motion in detail

The motion is the heart of the product, and it is built compliance-first on purpose. It is a three-step sequence: call → text → treat, and the sequence is named, in the code, "Dan core motion."

Step one is a call, and it is an inquiry, not a pitch. The distinction is a first-class concept in the system: a call is either an inquiry (a genuine, AI-disclosed, no-sell informational call whose only job is to earn consent for a follow-up, the lawful first touch) or a pitch (the actual sales conversation, only appropriate after consent is captured). Dan's first call is always an inquiry. The actual opening script is fixed and unambiguous:

"Hi, my name is Pam, I'm an AI assistant. I'm calling to ask whether {{name}} is currently hiring for sales or BDC roles, and if so, who's the best person to talk to."

Three things are load-bearing in that one sentence. Dan discloses he's an AI, immediately and by name. He asks a real, useful question, are you hiring?, rather than opening with a pitch. And he never tries to sell on that first call. The point of the inquiry is to find the right person and earn permission to follow up.

Steps two and three are consent-gated. Both the follow-up text and the gift carry a requiresConsent flag: they are blocked until consent was captured on the inquiry call. So the follow-up text, "Hi {{contactFirst}}, this is Dan with Pam following up on our call, thanks for the OK to reach out. Worth a 10-min chat this week?", cannot fire unless the dealer actually said yes. The gift, a coffee-and-doughnuts drop with a friendly note, waits two more days behind the same gate. The cadence is spaced (a day to the text, two more to the treat), and the whole sequence self-completes and cools off if there's no reply. The UI restates this everywhere it could matter: on the Outreach card ("Texts and treats wait until they're interested") and on the Prospect launcher.

Autopilot vs manual. The motion can run on autopilot, Dan places the calls, sends the texts, and triggers the gifts on cadence, paced so no dealer gets blasted and so a whole market isn't dialed at once. Or a rep can run it by hand: the Outreach card exposes an armed Call now / Text now / Send a treat button for the current step, and a Stop button beside it. The top-bar status cluster always tells the rep which world they're in, Dan's working vs Dan's paused, and You tap to call when manual calling is set. Whether Dan runs a step or the rep does, and whether the rep logs a touch by hand through Log a touch, it all writes to the same Activity timeline on the record. There is one history per dealer, and it does not care who or what produced each line.

That single-timeline design is the quiet backbone of the whole product. It is why Today's feed can honestly say "what Dan's been doing," why the CRM panel is trustworthy, and why a human and an AI can genuinely share an account without stepping on each other.

The provenance system

The provenance system is the product's answer to the oldest objection to any data tool: how do I know this is real? Dan's answer is not "trust us." It is "here's the source." The design comment says it in those words, and the UI lives up to it.

Mechanically, it is simple and everywhere. Any value that came from somewhere is wrapped so it renders with a dotted underline, and hovering it shows a small tooltip: Source: X · when. A phone on file reads "On file · OpenStreetMap / OEM record · as of Jun 30, 2026." A Google-filled phone reads "Google Places · checked live just now." A tech-stack vendor's tooltip shows the exact string that was matched on the dealer's site. A brand shows either "{OEM} dealer locator" when confirmed or "OpenStreetMap tag (unconfirmed)" when it isn't. The record even tells the rep the rule up front: "Hover any underlined value to see its source."

The most important discipline is the inferred-vs-sourced split. Values Dan fetched from a real source get that source named. Values Dan computed or guessed, dealer group, group size, territory, get a distinct, honest tooltip: "Estimated by Dan, inferred from other fields, not fetched from a source." They are never dressed up to look sourced. A rep can tell at a glance, on any field, whether they are looking at a fact someone can verify or an educated guess Dan made. That is the difference between a tool a rep trusts and one they quietly stop believing.

And sitting above all of it is the Raw / Enriched split, the global, persisted toggle that lets anyone drop the whole application to the raw scraped layer and see exactly what was pulled, with nothing cleaned or filled on top. Provenance tooltips answer "where did this one value come from"; the Raw/Enriched toggle answers "show me everything, unpolished." Together they make Dan a product a skeptic can audit rather than a black box they have to take on faith.

The honest assessment: this system is the most distinctive thing about Dan, and it is largely built and consistent. The tooltips are wired through the real screens, the inferred labeling is applied deliberately (not everywhere by reflex), and the Raw/Enriched mode genuinely swaps the data layer rather than just relabeling it. Where it is still rough is coverage completeness, not every single value in every card carries a tooltip yet, and a few "when" strings are relative ("checked live just now") rather than precise timestamps. But the pattern is right, it is applied where it matters most, and it sets a bar the rest of the product can be held to.

Part III

How Dan is built: the technology

This section is for the person who has to trust Dan before signing off on it: a CTO, an engineering lead at Pam, anyone whose name goes on the decision to put an autonomous outbound motion in front of real dealerships. It is not a feature tour. It is an account of the architecture, the specific safety and correctness mechanisms that back every claim the product makes, and an honest inventory of what is not yet hardened. Where a design choice trades one thing for another, that tradeoff is named. The goal is that after reading this you could open the repository and find that the code says what this document says it says.

Dan is two things that were built at different times and glued together without either one having to be rewritten. The lower half is a data layer: a census of roughly 34,000 franchise dealership rooftops across the US and Canada, assembled from public sources, verified, deduplicated, and scored. The upper half is a motion layer: a sequence engine that runs a compliance-first call-then-text-then-gift outreach cadence against those rooftops, through real voice and messaging providers, under a set of consent and human-in-the-loop locks. The interesting engineering is less in either half than in the seam between them, which was designed so the motion layer could be added later without editing a single line the data layer already depended on.

Architecture overview

Dan is a Next.js 15 application on the App Router, written in TypeScript end to end. The UI is React server components by default, with server actions for mutations, so the browser never holds a database handle or a provider credential. Styling is Tailwind with shadcn/Radix primitives. There is no separate API tier and no ORM-managed migration system in the request path. Persistence is a single local SQLite file (data/dealerships.sqlite) accessed through better-sqlite3, which is synchronous and in-process. For typed reads the code layers Drizzle over the same connection, but the schema of record is applied imperatively at open time rather than through a migration tool.

That last decision is worth dwelling on because it is load-bearing for the whole "two halves" story. lib/db.ts exposes getSqlite(), which lazily opens the database, sets journal_mode = WAL and foreign_keys = ON, and then runs a block of CREATE TABLE IF NOT EXISTS statements followed by a set of additive ALTER TABLE ... ADD COLUMN guards. The column guards read PRAGMA table_info(dealerships) into a Set and only add a column if it is missing. So every integration that came after the initial dealerships table (the HubSpot mirror columns, the flexible enrichment JSON blob, the cross-source sources/confirmation_count/trust_tier provenance fields) was introduced as an idempotent, forward-only column add. There is no down-migration, no version table, and no separate migrate step to forget to run. The pipeline CLI and the web app both call getSqlite() and get a database that is correct by construction on first touch.

The pipeline itself is a tsx command-line program, pipeline/run.ts, with subcommands (ingest, normalize, dedupe, validate, enrich, places, provenance, tier, export, report, and all). Each subcommand is written to be idempotent and resumable: you can run all end to end, or run any single stage repeatedly, and the database converges to the same state. The web app never invokes the pipeline; the pipeline writes rows, the app reads and annotates them.

The pattern that let the motion layer land cleanly is what the code comments call, in effect, zero-shared-edit. When the sequence engine was added, it did not modify lib/crm.ts, lib/crm-constants.ts, or the original dealerships DDL. Instead, lib/sequence.ts uses raw better-sqlite3 directly (mirroring lib/crm.ts rather than importing its constants), lazily creates its own three tables (sequences, enrollments, sequence_step_runs) inside its own sdb() bootstrap the first time it is called, and writes its timeline entries straight into the existing activity table. Crucially, activity.kind is unconstrained TEXT in the schema, so the sequence layer defines its own activity vocabulary in lib/sequence-constants.ts rather than editing the CRM's kind enum. The same lazy-bootstrap pattern appears again in lib/connections.ts (the connections table) and lib/places.ts (the places_cache table). Each new capability owns its own tables, its own kinds, and its own bootstrap, and merges onto the shared spine (dealerships and activity) only through append-only writes. The benefit is a low-conflict extension model where a feature can be added or removed without a schema migration touching anyone else's rows. The cost, stated plainly, is that table creation is spread across several modules instead of centralized, and the "schema" is really the union of several lazy bootstraps rather than one file you can read top to bottom. For a single-file SQLite app maintained by a small team this is a reasonable trade; at larger scale it would want consolidating behind a real migration tool.

The data pipeline

The census is built from three independent classes of source, normalized into one master schema, then deduplicated, validated, tiered, and franchise-gated. The design principle throughout is that no single source is trusted alone; a rooftop's credibility is a function of how many independent sources agree on it.

The backbone is OpenStreetMap, queried through the Overpass API (pipeline/sources/osm.ts). The query is deliberately narrow: only branded car shops, expressed in Overpass QL as node["shop"="car"]["brand"] and the equivalent way, so independents and used lots without a brand tag are dropped at the source. The hard part of OSM at national scale is not the query but the request shape. Overpass endpoints time out on large areas, so the source first attempts a single admin-boundary query per region keyed on the ISO3166-2 code, and only if that fails does it fall back to bounding-box tiling. The tiler is adaptive: it issues a tile, and if the server responds with a remark matching /timed out|runtime error|too many|memory/, it recursively splits the box into four quadrants down to a MIN_TILE_DEG floor of 0.75 degrees. Dense states carry smaller starting tiles. Every query is tried across a list of Overpass mirrors in order. OSM rooftops are marked brand_confirmed: false in the record, because an OSM brand tag is a hint, not a confirmation of an active franchise relationship.

The second class is OEM dealer-locator adapters (pipeline/sources/oem/). These are the authoritative source when they work, because a hit on Ford's own locator is by definition a confirmed Ford franchise, so these records are marked brand_confirmed: true. The adapter framework is declarative: createOemSource in base.ts takes a config that only has to describe how to turn a geo grid point into an HTTP request (buildRequest) and how to parse the JSON response (parse); the base handles the grid walk, per-point caching, within-source dedup on a stable dealer id where present, rate limiting, and retries.

Here the document has to be honest about a real-world reality rather than a design intention. Many OEM locator endpoints sit behind Akamai or AWS WAF bot protection and refuse automated traffic outright. The adapters are correct, and they carry realistic browser headers and referers, but from a normal server IP most requests come back refused or as an HTML challenge page rather than JSON. The base source counts okPoints versus blockedPoints and, when a grid produced points but zero of them parsed, logs an explicit note that the endpoint is blocking automated traffic and that the adapter needs a non-blocked IP or proxy to populate. The mitigation is a proxy path: pipeline/config.ts exposes http.proxyUrl (from PROXY_URL), and the HTTP client in pipeline/lib/http.ts builds an undici ProxyAgent that OEM adapters opt into via useProxy: true. Pointed at a residential-unblocking proxy such as Bright Data's Web Unlocker, the same adapters that returned zero from a datacenter IP return populated JSON. This is not a workaround bolted on after the fact; the proxy dispatcher and the per-request useProxy flag are first-class in the HTTP layer. But it should be stated clearly as a known limitation: without a proxy, OEM coverage is effectively unavailable, and the OEM path remains WAF-blocked from an ordinary network. It is a hardening follow-up, not a solved problem.

Because OEM coverage cannot be relied on unassisted, the reliable path to full coverage is the Google Places coverage engine (runPlacesDiscover, which lives in the sibling data-pipeline repo). Google already holds the comprehensive list of businesses, so the engine enumerates rather than crawls: it builds a geographic grid over the enabled regions, and for each brand-and-grid-point pair it issues a Text Search for "{brand} dealer" biased to a circle around that point (default 45 km radius, 0.7-degree grid step). It paginates up to three pages per cell via nextPageToken, runs a bounded-concurrency pool (default twelve workers) over the network fetches, and dedups on Google's canonical place_id, which is free, exact, and stable, so re-running the engine is idempotent. Each candidate is filtered twice before it is allowed in: the display name must actually contain the brand token, and it must not match a large NOISE regular expression that strips service and parts departments, body and collision shops, powersports, marine, RV, rental, quick-lane, and other non-franchise sub-listings. A surviving place is either merged onto an existing rooftop that shares its computed dedup key (appending google_places to that rooftop's source set) or inserted net-new. The engine also reports estimated spend, since Text Search is billed per request; the code notes the roughly $32-per-thousand rate and prints an estimated dollar cost at the end of a run. This is the workhorse: OSM gives a free backbone, OEM gives authoritative confirmation where reachable, and Places gives the comprehensive coverage that turns a partial list into a census.

All three feed a common shape. pipeline/steps/normalize.ts maps each raw record to a validated MasterRecord (rejecting anything without a name), derives a clean domain from the website, and computes a dedupKey. The dedup key is a precedence ladder in buildDedupKey: OEM plus normalized street address if a street is present, else OEM plus rounded lat/lng, else OEM plus domain, else OEM plus a normalized name. Dedup then runs in two passes: pass one collapses exact key collisions, and pass two folds domain-only records (a locator hit with no street) onto an address-keyed record for the same OEM and domain, so an OSM rooftop and an OEM-locator record for the same store reconcile into one. Merges are not naive: OEM-sourced fields win over OSM, brand_confirmed is sticky (logical OR), tool and contact lists union, and the source strings join into a +-separated set. Validation checks phone validity with libphonenumber-js against the record's country and probes websites for liveness. Tiering classifies rooftops A/B. The franchise gate is effectively enforced at three points working together: OSM's brand-tag requirement at query time, the Places noise filter and brand-token requirement at ingest, and the canonicalizeOem step that only admits recognized OEM brands. Independents never enter the census in the first place.

Enrichment and verification

Coverage answers "does this rooftop exist." Enrichment answers "can Dan actually reach it, and can we stand behind the value we show." The governing philosophy across this layer is precision-first: a wrong value is worse than a blank one, because a rep who dials a wrong number or pitches the wrong store loses trust in the whole tool. Every mechanism here is biased toward refusing to fill a field rather than filling it wrong.

The live verification path is lib/places.ts, which is server-only and sits behind its own cache table (places_cache), one row per rooftop. verifyRooftop first reads the cache; the cache read is TTL-aware, computed in SQL as julianday('now') - julianday(fetched_at), and treats anything older than 30 days as a miss so the paid Google API is hit at most once per rooftop per month. This is what lets an offline or demo run still render whatever was last verified, rather than going blank. If there is no key configured, the function returns null and the caller renders nothing; the feature degrades cleanly and never blocks a page. The fetch itself uses a tight AbortSignal.timeout(4000) so a slow API can never stall a server render, and a restrictive field mask so Google only bills for the fields Dan uses.

Two of the correctness details in this file deserve emphasis. The first is negative caching: when Google returns no match, the code writes a row with found = 0 and the reader returns null for it, so a rooftop Google genuinely does not know is not re-queried on every render. Only transient network or API errors are left uncached, because those are worth retrying. The second is the 12 km haversine distance gate. Text Search biased to a location can happily return a same-brand dealer in the next town over. Returning that store's phone number would be a precision failure of exactly the kind the whole layer exists to prevent. So fetchFromGoogle computes the haversine distance between the rooftop's known coordinates and the matched place's coordinates, and if it exceeds MAX_MATCH_KM = 12 it rejects the match entirely and returns null. The inline comment is the design in one sentence: a far match is a different rooftop of the same brand, and a wrong phone is worse than none. This gate is the practical answer to the single most common way a location-biased search silently corrupts data.

Verification is also the phone-backfill mechanism. A large majority of rooftops, on the order of 80 percent, arrive from OSM and the coverage engine without a phone number, because neither OSM tags nor Places Text Search reliably carry one. The Places verification pulls nationalPhoneNumber, and the fill is strictly additive: in the pipeline's cross-confirm step, updates use COALESCE(phone, @phone) so an existing confirmed value is never overwritten, only a missing one is filled. Same for address and website. The result is that a phone number Dan shows either came from a source that had it or from a Google-verified match that passed the distance gate; it is never a guess.

The HubSpot reconciliation engine

lib/hubspot.ts exists so a rep who already runs a HubSpot pipeline sees their real deal state overlaid on Dan's rooftops, without Dan ever writing to their HubSpot. The direction of data flow is a hard invariant: read-only on HubSpot, writes only into Dan. Reconciliation runs from a server action under the rep's own private-app token, which is why the module is self-contained on raw better-sqlite3 rather than reaching for a global config.

The engine pulls the rep's owners, companies, and contacts (paginated, capped), then matches each HubSpot company to a Dan rooftop, and the matcher is where the precision philosophy gets its most careful expression. Matching a CRM to a census is a classic false-positive trap: two dealers that share only the words "Auto Group" or only the brand "Nissan" are not the same store, and attaching one rep's deal to the wrong rooftop is a quietly corrosive error. So the matcher refuses to treat generic and brand words as identity. It maintains a GENERIC set ("auto", "group", "motors", "sales", directional words, and so on) and a BRANDS set (every OEM name and nickname), and distinctive() strips both, leaving only the tokens that actually identify a specific business. A name match requires distinctive-token agreement: two distinctive tokens in common, or one plus a city-name hit. Domain is treated as the reliable identity key and tried first, but even a domain match is not blindly trusted: if several rooftops share one group domain, the code disambiguates by brand and city before accepting one, and falls through to name matching rather than guessing when a shared domain stays ambiguous.

Two guards make this robust rather than merely plausible. Brand-conflict rejection: brandConflict extracts the brands named in the HubSpot company name and, if the rooftop carries a brand that does not intersect them, rejects the candidate. So "Hall Chrysler Dodge Jeep Ram" will not match a Toyota rooftop even if other tokens line up. City-token exclusion: when scoring a name match, the rooftop's own city is deleted from its distinctive tokens, so a real name token has to carry the match. The comment gives the concrete failure this prevents: without it, "Jerry's Ford Alexandria" and "Ourisman Ford of Alexandria" would match on "Alexandria" alone, which is wrong. Matched companies get their lifecycle stage (cleaned through a LIFECYCLE map that also collapses opaque custom numeric stage ids to "In pipeline" rather than showing a raw number), deal owner, and last activity written into Dan's mirror columns; matched contacts are merged onto the rooftop, with prior HubSpot-sourced contacts refreshed and de-duplicated by email or name. The whole company overlay runs inside a single db.transaction. Precision matters here for the same reason it matters in verification, only worse: a mis-attributed deal doesn't just show a blank, it shows a confident lie about where an account stands.

ZoomInfo intelligence

The census gets Dan to the right store; ZoomInfo gets Dan to the right person's actual cell phone. The integration takes the decision-makers Dan already found by name on staff pages and enriches them to a direct dial and verified email, so a rep calls the GM's mobile rather than the front desk. The intended path is the GTM MCP flow of company search, contact search, and contact enrich, requesting directPhone, mobilePhone, email, and title, then preferring direct or mobile over the main line.

Everything about this integration is built to protect credits, because ZoomInfo is metered and expensive. It is a dry run unless ZOOMINFO_APPLY=1, and a dry run makes no API calls and spends nothing; it only reports how many credits a real run would consume. It is scoped to a region list, capped at a maximum account count, and it selects candidates highest-Pam-fit-first with an optional fit floor, so the credits that are spent land on the best-fit accounts rather than being burned uniformly. Only rooftops that already have named staff-page contacts are even considered, because enriching a person you cannot name is pointless.

The essential correctness requirement, and the one the CTO reading this should probe hardest, is domain-verified attribution. Every enrich input carries companyName and companyDomain, and results are merged back onto the specific rooftop they came from. The failure mode if attribution is loose is severe and specific: ZoomInfo can return a real person with a real direct dial who works at a different dealership in the same group or the same metro, and if that contact is stapled onto the wrong rooftop, Dan will hand a rep a confident, correct-looking phone number for the wrong store. That is the ZoomInfo-shaped version of the same precision failure the distance gate and the brand-conflict check exist to prevent, and it is why enrichment must be keyed on the company domain rather than on name similarity alone. One operational note for this environment: the ZoomInfo MCP connector requires authorization that cannot be completed in a non-interactive session, so a rep has to connect it through their own connector settings before the enrich path is live; until then the integration stays in its safe, credit-free dry-run state.

The motion engine

The motion engine is a small, explicit state machine over three tables and one activity log, and its most important properties are the ones that keep it from doing something irreversible. The tables are sequences (a named cadence, its steps stored as JSON), enrollments (one rooftop's progress through one sequence), and sequence_step_runs (the record of each executed step). The canonical cadence, DAN_SEQUENCE_STEPS in lib/sequence-constants.ts, is compliance-first by construction: step one is an AI-disclosed, no-sell inquiry call whose only job is to ask whether the store is hiring and who handles it, and thereby to earn consent; steps two (a text) and three (a doughnuts gift) both carry requiresConsent: true and are the actual follow-up. The data model encodes the compliance posture, it is not just a UI convention.

Enrollment is idempotent: enroll returns the existing active enrollment if one exists, and a partial unique index (enrollments_active_uniq on (dealership_id, sequence_id) WHERE state = 'active') enforces at most one active enrollment per rooftop per sequence at the database level. Segment enrollment can enroll a whole brand-and-geography slice at once, but only over rooftops that actually have a phone, capped, and with staggered next_run_at timestamps so a batch does not fire simultaneously. Scheduling is delay-based: each step carries a Delay (value plus unit), delayMs converts it to milliseconds, and advance schedules the next step at completion-time plus that delay, or completes the enrollment when no steps remain.

The scheduler is tick in pipeline/sequence/tick.ts, one pass over due enrollments, written to be idempotent and resumable so it is safe on a cron. watch.ts is the autopilot loop that calls tick and then syncCallOutcomes on an interval and records a heartbeat the UI reads. Within a tick, several gates fire in order, and they are the substance of why this engine can be trusted to run itself:

A global kill switch: if SEQUENCE_ENABLED=0, the tick is a no-op immediately. An exit guard: if the rooftop's CRM status is engaged, won, or lost, the enrollment exits, because a live deal should stop the robot. Idempotency: a step whose run row is already sent is never re-dispatched; the enrollment simply advances past it. This, combined with the unique index on (enrollment_id, step_index), means a step is dispatched at most once even if a tick runs twice.

Then the two locks that matter most. The human-initiated call lock: an autonomous tick will never dial a real number. If the step is a call and the tick was not flagged humanInitiated (the flag a "Call" button click sets), the tick will only proceed if SEQ_TEST_TO is set, in which case the call rings the rep's own test line, or if SEQ_AUTONOMOUS_CALLS=1 is explicitly set as a post-counsel override. Otherwise the call is held and rescheduled an hour out. A real dealership's phone is never auto-dialed; only a human click, a test line, or a deliberate override places a real call. The consent gate: any step with requiresConsent is blocked unless the CRM status is engaged, meaning consent was captured on the inquiry call; until then the step is held and re-checked hourly. A sales message is never sent to someone who did not opt in. There is a gift-budget rail as well: a gift over the per-gift cap, or one that would push cumulative gift spend over the budget, is skipped rather than sent.

Channel routing (pipeline/sequence/channels/index.ts) fails safe to simulation. If SEQUENCE_APPLY is not 1, every channel resolves to the simulated adapter, which logs what it would send, charges nothing, and returns a deterministic fake reference, so the whole engine is runnable and demo-able with no provider keys at all. Even with SEQUENCE_APPLY=1, if the rep's chosen provider is not actually configured, routing logs a warning and falls back to simulation rather than failing a send in a surprising way. When it is configured, calls route to the rep's chosen voice provider (Vapi, Bland, or Twilio TTS) with a fallback to whichever voice provider has credentials, texts route to Twilio, and gifts to the gifting provider. Every real adapter honors the same test overrides: SEQ_TEST_TO redirects calls and texts to the rep's own number, and SEQ_TEST_ADDRESS redirects gifts, so the first real send always goes to yourself before it goes to a rooftop. Each dispatch is recorded through recordStepRun, an idempotent upsert that also increments an attempt counter. Call outcomes are synced back separately: listUnsyncedCallRuns finds sent calls without an outcome yet, and the sync path writes the provider's transcript summary back onto the run. The one deliberately blunt policy in the MVP is that a failed step is dropped and the motion advances rather than retrying, so a single failure never stalls a cadence; that is a conscious simplicity choice, noted as such in the code.

Security and multi-tenancy

Provider credentials are per-rep and never live in a shared .env in production use. lib/connections.ts stores each credential in a connections table keyed on (rep_id, name), and lib/secrets.ts encrypts every value at rest with AES-256-GCM. The key comes from APP_SECRET (run through scryptSync) if set, otherwise a random 32-byte key generated once and persisted under data/cache/conn.key, which is gitignored; keys are never stored in plaintext in the database. Each stored blob is iv:tag:ciphertext, and decryption verifies the GCM auth tag and returns an empty string on any failure rather than throwing, so a corrupt or wrong-key value degrades to "not configured" instead of crashing a request.

The runtime read path is repEnv(repId), which decrypts that rep's saved credentials and merges them over process.env, with the database winning. Every part of the motion engine and the live verifier reads through repEnv(), so "this rep's keys" is the natural unit and .env is only ever a fallback for local development. Display is masked: connectionStatus shows secrets as bullets plus the last four characters and reports connection state from the effective value, so the UI can tell a rep a provider is connected without ever revealing the secret back to the browser. Today there is a single implicit rep (DEFAULT_REP_ID = 1) because login has not landed yet, but the schema is already keyed by rep_id throughout, so real multi-tenant login is a drop-in rather than a rewrite. That is the honest status: the isolation boundary is designed and the storage is encrypted; the authentication that would make it a true multi-tenant system is a known follow-up.

Provenance and the Raw/Enriched system

The claim that underwrites the whole product is that every value Dan shows carries a defensible source. Provenance is the mechanism. Each rooftop's source field is a +-joined set of the independent sources that confirmed it, and pipeline/steps/provenance.ts turns that into a sources[] array, a confirmation_count, and a trust_tier: three or more independent confirmations is platinum, two is gold, one is silver, zero is flagged. Because OSM, each OEM locator, and Google Places all append their own token to source, the tier is a direct readout of how many genuinely independent systems agree a given store exists as described. This is what lets Dan show not just a rooftop but a confidence in it, and it is why the coverage engine appends google_places to an existing rooftop's source set when it confirms one rather than silently overwriting.

That provenance surfaces to the rep through a product-wide Raw versus Enriched mode. components/data-view-toggle.tsx is a small client component that sets data-view on the <html> element and persists the choice in localStorage under dan-view. The rendering is pure CSS: two rules in app/globals.css hide .enriched-only under [data-view="raw"] and hide .raw-only under any non-raw view, so any element can opt into being visible in only one mode, and the entire app flips between Dan's enriched, verified layer and the raw scraped source with a single toggle. It is one product showing both layers, not two pages. To avoid a flash of the wrong layer on load, the mode is applied before paint: a small inline script in app/layout.tsx reads dan-view from localStorage and sets document.documentElement.dataset.view synchronously in the document head, alongside the same pre-paint trick for dark mode. The point of the toggle is trust: a skeptical rep, or a skeptical buyer, can flip to Raw and see exactly what was scraped underneath every enriched value, which is only a defensible offer because the provenance data behind it is real.

Known limitations and hardening follow-ups

None of the above is finished software, and the case for trusting it is stronger if the gaps are named rather than smoothed over. The consent gate keys on CRM status reaching engaged rather than on a per-enrollment consent flag, which is a coarser signal than it should be; a dedicated per-enrollment consent record is a planned tightening. The gift-budget rail meters against catalog cost estimates and simulated cost, and does not yet reconcile against a provider's real post-send charge, so the budget accounting is approximate until a cost fallback that reads actual charges lands. The OEM locator adapters remain WAF-blocked from ordinary networks and depend on a residential-unblocking proxy to populate at all, which means OEM coverage is operationally gated on proxy availability rather than being self-sufficient. And the entire data layer is a periodic batch, not a live feed: the app reads whatever the last pipeline run wrote, so freshness is bounded by how often the pipeline is run, and there is no streaming update path yet. Authentication is not implemented, so the per-rep isolation is real in the schema but not yet enforced by login. These are the right things to fix next, and stating them is part of what should make the rest of this document credible: the mechanisms that are described as working are backed by code you can read, and the parts that are not yet hardened are called out as such rather than dressed up.

Part IV

The feel, the UX, the honest state, and the road ahead

The feel, and why it feels that way

Open Dan and the first thing you notice is what is missing. No dashboard wall of numbers. No red badges competing for attention. No gradient hero telling you that you are a sales champion. What you get instead is a warm paper-cream canvas, a single calm sentence, and one card that says who to call and why. That restraint is the product. Everything else is downstream of it.

Dan is built for one person: a franchise-dealership sales rep who is on the road most of the day, who does not think of himself as a "user," and who has been burned by every CRM he has ever been handed. He is not going to read a manual. He is going to glance at a phone between a parking lot and a service drive, decide something, and put the phone back in his pocket. If the interface makes him stop and think, we have already lost. So the entire design is organized around a single question the rep is always silently asking: who do I call, why, and what do I say. When Dan answers that in one glance, it works. When it does not, nothing else about it matters.

Warm and calm, on purpose

The palette is cream and clay. The canvas is a warm paper tone, the ink is a soft warm near-black rather than pure black, and there is exactly one accent color: a clay terracotta that carries the brand and marks the one thing on the screen worth acting on. Green and red exist, but they are reserved. They mean something semantic (verified, invalid, closed) and nothing decorative. When a screen has only one clay button, that button is unambiguous: it is the next action. The moment we let clay leak onto three buttons, the screen would lose its center of gravity, so we do not.

Type does quiet work here too. Page titles are set in Newsreader, a serif, at medium weight with slightly tightened tracking. Everything else is Inter. The serif is not decoration; it is a signal that this is an editorial, considered surface, not a data-entry grid. It slows the eye down by a fraction of a second in exactly the places where slowing down is good, the titles, and gets out of the way everywhere else. The radius is generous, the whitespace is generous, and motion is deliberately small: content enters with a six-pixel rise over about a quarter second, hot items pulse once softly, buttons compress very slightly when pressed. All of it respects the reduced-motion preference. The goal is a surface that feels alive without ever feeling busy, the Apple and Anthropic instinct that calm is a feature, not an absence of features.

There is a real reason the calm is load-bearing and not just taste. A rep works Dan under cognitive load, in a car, half-distracted, often anxious about a number. An anxious interface compounds an anxious moment. A calm one absorbs it. When Dan has nothing urgent, the Today screen does not invent urgency to justify its own existence. It shows a coffee cup and says "You're all caught up." That is a design decision with a spine: we would rather show an honest empty state than manufacture a fake task to keep the rep engaged. Engagement-for-its-own-sake is the CRM disease. Dan refuses it.

One hook per card, and label everything

Every card in Dan carries exactly one idea. The Do-Next card on Today is a dealership name, a one-line reason, and a Call button. That is the whole card. The territory list is a numbered row, a name, an OEM. The "Who to call and why" card gives one person to call first, one reason, one opener line. We resist the urge to pack a card with six facts because a card with six facts has no hook, and a rep scanning at a glance cannot tell what he is supposed to do with it. One hook per card means one decision per card, and a stack of one-decision cards is a screen a tired person can actually read.

Alongside that, we label everything, because the rep is always a first-time viewer. Not literally, but functionally: he opens Dan a few times a day, in different states of attention, and he should never have to remember what a word means. So the record page has a small line under the header, "Hover any underlined value to see its source." Tier and "In HubSpot" and "Contacts" each carry a tiny info tip that explains the term in one plain sentence. The stepper labels each dot Call, Text, Treat rather than trusting an icon alone. We assume no accumulated knowledge. Anything that would require the rep to have learned something gets a label instead.

Trust comes from "here's the source," not "trust us"

This is the design principle the whole product is built on, and it is worth being precise about because it shapes almost every component. Sales data is usually sold on authority: our database says this dealership's phone number is X, believe us. Reps have learned, correctly, to distrust that, because they have all called a "verified" number that rang a fax machine in 2014. So Dan does the opposite. It never asks to be trusted. It shows its work.

Concretely: almost every meaningful value on a record is wrapped so that hovering it reveals exactly where the value came from and when. The phone number reads "On file, OpenStreetMap / OEM record, as of Jun 30, 2026." The rating reads "Google Places, checked live just now." The address carries "OpenStreetMap / dealer record" and, when Google confirms it, a second line that says so. Every data group has a source tag, and where a source is verifiable, the tag is a link: click "Source: OpenStreetMap" and it opens the map centered on the rooftop so the rep can confirm the building with his own eyes. The provenance component is written so it can only ever state a source the system can actually defend. It does not invent provenance. If there is no defensible source, there is no tooltip.

That principle forces an honesty most tools skip. Dan distinguishes, visibly and always, between three kinds of value: sourced (fetched from somewhere nameable), inferred (computed by Dan from other fields), and live (checked against Google in this moment). Dealer group and group size and territory are labeled "Estimated by Dan, inferred from other fields, not fetched from a source." That is a small confession on every record, and it is the most trust-building thing in the product. A rep who sees Dan admit "I guessed this one" will believe Dan the moment it says "this one is confirmed." Tools that never admit a guess get disbelieved on everything.

Honesty as a design value

The provenance system is the biggest expression of an honesty stance, but the stance runs deeper than one component.

Blank beats wrong. When Dan has no named contact, it does not fabricate a plausible one or show a stale scrape. It shows a dashed box that says "No names yet, call the main line and ask who runs the sales floor." When there is no reason to call yet, the record says "Nothing jumping out yet, call and find out what they're working with." Empty states are written as honest, useful instructions, not as failures to paper over. A wrong contact costs the rep a real phone call and a real embarrassment; a blank costs him nothing and tells him exactly what to do next.

Validation never alarms without cause. The record's validation panel is careful about a distinction most tools fatten over: not-yet-checked is not the same as wrong. A phone that has not been validated shows a neutral "Not yet verified," never a red "Invalid." Only a number that affirmatively failed validation goes red. Brand on a branded store is either "Confirmed" or "Not yet confirmed," never a scary red flag, because a Honda store with an unconfirmed brand tag is not a problem, it is just un-double-checked. Red in Dan means something is actually wrong, so red keeps its meaning.

No gamified cringe, and no internal marketing to the rep. There are no streaks, no confetti, no "You're crushing it!" The one goal card states a number plainly. When Dan has been working leads in the background, Today says "What Dan's been doing" and lists them, flatly. We never sell the rep on Dan inside Dan. The tool's job is to be useful, not to convince the person using it that it is useful. Reps can smell internal marketing, and it reads as a substitute for substance.

How this reduces cognitive load

Put the pieces together and the load-reduction is structural, not cosmetic. The rep never has to hold state in his head. Today tells him the single next action. The record answers who-to-call (one named person, or the main line if there is no name yet), why-to-call (a short reason drawn from real signals), and what-to-say (an opener line, printed, ready to read aloud). The provenance answers the doubt that would otherwise make him hesitate. The one clay button per screen answers "what do I press." Each of these removes a small decision, and small decisions are what exhaust a person working forty rooftops. The measure of the design is not that it looks nice. It is that a rep can open it in a service lane, know what to do in one glance, do it, and close it, and never once feel managed.

The UX spine: one motion, not a pile of features

A tool can have every right feature and still feel like a pile, because the features do not connect into a motion the rep already has in his body. Dan's navigation is deliberately small, five destinations, and named in the rep's language rather than the software's: Today, Prospect, Territory, Book, with Connections and Integrations tucked under a quiet Setup heading. Book, not Accounts. Prospect, not Sequences. Territory, not Map. The words are chosen so a rep who has never seen the app can guess what each does. But the real work is in how the four main screens hand off to each other so the whole thing feels like one continuous gesture: decide, go, work, record.

Today decides

Today is the front door and it has exactly one job: answer "what do I do first." The screen leads with a single Do-Next card, the hottest lead, its name, a why-now sentence, and a full-width Call-now button, with a Snooze control for when the timing is wrong. Below it, "Then these" is a compact list of the rest of the queue, each row just enough to decide on, not a second full card competing for the eye. Below that, the goal, stated plainly, and "What Dan's been doing," the background activity feed. The information hierarchy is the message: the next action is the biggest thing on the screen, and a statistic is never allowed to be bigger than an action. A rep opens Today, reads one card, and taps Call. That is the intended interaction, and most mornings it is the whole interaction.

Territory routes

The rep is mobile, so the second question after "who" is "who is near me." Territory answers it. Pick a city and Dan clusters every dealer in that area onto a live OpenStreetMap view, plotting them as numbered pins in a route order it has already computed, clay pins for dealers already in the pipeline, gray for not-yet-contacted, and a live blue "you are here" dot that follows the rep's device as he drives. Today's calendar appointments drop onto the same map. Underneath, two plain lists: "In your pipeline, check in while you're here" and "Not yet contacted, worth a stop." The framing is the rep's own: "Where are you today? Dan clusters your dealers by area so you can see everyone in one trip, in an efficient order." Territory turns a scattered book of business into a drive. It is the bridge between the decision Today made and the physical day the rep is actually going to have.

The record works

Tap any dealership, from Today, from Territory, from Book, and you land on the same record, and the record is where a call actually gets made. It opens with the name (wrapped in provenance), status, tier, a trust badge showing how many independent sources confirmed the rooftop, and whether it is already in HubSpot. Then the part that earns the screen: a "Why call" block with a live Google-verified strip (rating, review count, open/closed, phone), a "Who to call and why" card naming one person to call first with a printed opener line, and Dan's outreach plan, the call-text-treat stepper showing what has been sent and what is next. If Google says the rooftop is permanently closed, a red banner says so before the rep wastes a drive on it. Everything a rep needs to place one good call is above the fold, and everything below it, identity, validation, map, tech stack, signals, is there for the rep who wants to go deeper, in that order of importance, never in the way.

Logging a touch, and the sync

Here is the hinge that makes it a motion instead of a form. After the call, the rep logs it in one tap. The Log-a-touch control is three pills, Log call, Log text, Log gift, and tapping one opens a single free-text field with a plain-language placeholder ("Are they hiring? Who should you talk to? e.g. GM is Jane Ruiz") and an Enter-to-save. One line, one tap, done. And critically, the note under it: "Logs to this dealer's timeline. Dan's automated touches land here too, one history, whether you run it or he does." That single sentence is the reason the motion holds together. The rep's manual call and Dan's autonomous call land on the same timeline. There is not a "my activity" and a "the robot's activity." There is one history of this relationship, and it does not matter who moved it forward. From there the record's CRM state, status, owner, next step, activity, is what syncs to HubSpot, so the rep's own system of record stays current without him ever opening HubSpot to type it in.

Why it feels usable to someone who hates CRMs

Reps hate CRMs for a specific reason: a CRM asks the rep to feed it. It is a hungry form that turns a salesperson into a data-entry clerk, and it gives nothing back in the moment. Dan inverts the contract. It feeds the rep, who to call, why, what to say, and it takes as little back as it can, one tap, one optional line. The logging is a byproduct of the work, not a separate chore stacked on top of it. The rep never has to leave Dan to make the call (the number is a tap-to-dial link), never has to open Maps (Territory is the map), never has to open HubSpot (the sync is automatic). Every bounce to another app is a small failure the design is actively trying to prevent, which is exactly why the ride-along instrument the team uses treats "left Dan for another app" as the single most important thing to count. The spine works when the rep can run his whole day, decide, drive, call, log, without the tool ever making him stop and manage the tool.

The honest state of the product

A product leader earns trust by being precise about what is real, what is rough, and what matters most next. Here is that assessment, without spin.

What genuinely works today

The data is real and it is substantial. Dan's book of business is roughly 34,700 franchise rooftops across the US, Canada, and Mexico (the last behind a flag). The overwhelming majority are brand-confirmed against an OEM source, the overwhelming majority carry a phone number (a large share of them Google-verified and backfilled where the raw sources had none), and every rooftop with coordinates is mappable. This is not a demo dataset. It is a real North American franchise-dealer system of record, built from OpenStreetMap as the backbone plus OEM dealer locators and a Google Places coverage engine, deduped on OEM-plus-normalized-address, validated for website and phone and brand, and tiered. The pipeline that produces it is idempotent and resumable, each step runs on its own, raw responses are cached, and nothing is fabricated: when an OEM locator sits behind bot protection and returns nothing, the adapter degrades to zero and logs it rather than inventing records.

Territory is genuinely good. The clustering, the route-ordered numbered pins, the live location dot, the calendar overlay, this is the feature that most makes Dan feel like it belongs to a person who drives for a living. It is the clearest expression of the product's thesis that the rep is mobile and the tool should meet him where his body actually is.

The record is strong. The "Why call," "Who to call and why," opener line, and live Google verification strip turn a row in a database into something a rep can act on cold. The distinction between on-file data and live-checked data is handled honestly and visibly. This is the surface where the product's design principles are most fully realized.

The provenance system is the crown jewel. It is not a feature bolted on; it is the thing that makes every other feature believable. Sourced-versus-inferred-versus-live is threaded through the entire record. The trust tier, platinum for multi-source-confirmed rooftops down to flagged, is computed from how many independent sources agree, and it is shown, not hidden. This is the part of the product a competitor cannot cheaply copy, because copying it means rebuilding your entire data stance around defensibility.

The motion engine works end to end in simulation. The call-text-treat sequence, the enrollment model, the idempotent tick scheduler, the one-history timeline, all of it runs, dry-run by default, with real safety rails already in the code: a global kill switch, the human-initiated call lock, a per-gift and a total gift budget, per-tick caps, and hard exit conditions so Dan stops touching a live deal the moment a human marks it engaged. The status bar tells the rep the truth about which mode he is in: "Practice mode" versus "Calling for real," with an explicit "You tap to call" chip when autonomous calling is off.

What is still rough

The franchise gate and dedup are not yet fully finished on the full dataset. The dataset is real and usable, but it is not yet fully gated to franchise-only and fully deduped at 34K scale. Until that pass is done, a small fraction of rooftops may be independents that slipped the brand filter, or near-duplicate rooftops (one physical store split into several franchise rows) that should be merged. This is the single most important correctness item, because everything downstream, who to call, the routes, the trust tiers, inherits from the quality of the underlying records.

Contacts are thin. Only a minority of rooftops carry a named contact today, from a free website-scrape enricher plus targeted ZoomInfo runs. That is honest and it is a real limit. For most rooftops, "Who to call" correctly falls back to "call the main line and ask who runs the sales floor," which is the right behavior, but it means the who-to-call promise is only partially kept today. Named-contact coverage is where paid enrichment would move the needle most, and it is deliberately metered rather than turned on wholesale, because a wrong contact is worse than none.

Some enrichment is live-per-request rather than stored. The Google verification on the record is fetched live and cached with a TTL, which is great for freshness but means it is not yet a durable field the rest of the app can filter or route on. The line between what is scraped-and-stored and what is checked-live is drawn correctly for honesty, but it has product consequences: you cannot yet build a route out of "open on Google right now."

The trust and data-credibility work that matters most

If I were prioritizing one theme, it is this: the data has to be right, and the product must never contradict itself about it. Two specific commitments follow.

First, contacts must be right. A wrong name on a record is worse than no name, because the rep acts on it and burns credibility on a live call. Every contact must carry a source and a date, and anything below a confidence bar should be shown as a pattern ("email pattern: first.last@") rather than asserted as a fact, and a contact must be verifiably attached to the specific rooftop, not merely the same brand in the same metro. Blank beats wrong is not a slogan here; it is the acceptance criterion for the contacts feature.

Second, validation must never contradict itself. The most corrosive thing a data tool can do is say a rooftop is high-trust in one place and show an invalid phone in another, or verify an address in the header while the map points somewhere else. The provenance system already fights this by making every value name its own source, but as more sources come in (Google, ZoomInfo, HubSpot, the dealer's own site), the reconciliation logic has to be airtight: when two sources disagree, Dan must show both and say which it trusts and why, not silently pick one and hide the conflict. Consistency across the record is the whole ballgame. The moment the rep catches Dan contradicting itself, the provenance system stops buying trust and starts costing it.

That is the mature read: the bones are real and the design stance is right, the roughness is concentrated in dataset completion and contact depth, and the work that matters most is keeping the product's honesty airtight as its inputs multiply.

How it goes to the next level

The roadmap below is sequenced by dependency, not wish. Near-term items unblock mid-term ones; the long-term vision is only credible because the near-term work makes it so. For each step I note roughly how hard it is, because a roadmap without effort estimates is just a wish list.

Near term: finish the foundation and harden the motion

1. Finish the franchise gate and dedup on the full 34K. (Moderate, mostly pipeline work, no new systems.) Run the brand-confirmation and dedup passes to completion across all rooftops: gate out the independents that slipped the OSM brand filter, merge the near-duplicate rooftops on OEM-plus-address with domain fallback, and resolve the unconfirmed-brand records into confirmed-or-dropped. This unlocks everything downstream, because trust tiers, routes, and counts are only as good as the record set they run on. It is the highest-leverage near-term work precisely because it is unglamorous.

2. Per-enrollment consent and budget rails on the live motion. (Moderate; the rails partly exist, the gap is the consent gate and the surfacing.) Before Dan can call for real at scale, every enrollment needs an explicit, logged consent decision (rather than keying on the shared CRM status), and the existing gift and per-tick budgets need to be visible in the UI, not just in env vars. The status bar already tells the rep which mode he is in; the next step is a spend-and-scope panel that makes the guardrails legible: how many calls today, how much gift budget remains, which rooftops are enrolled. This unlocks turning real sending on with confidence instead of nerve.

3. Unify "who to call" into one resolved answer. (Moderate.) Today, contact, main line, Google phone, and OEM record are reconciled at render time on the record, and two different ranking systems can still disagree between the worklist and the opener. Promote that into one stored, sourced "best contact and best number" per rooftop, with the conflict logic explicit, so every surface leads with the same person and the same number. This is the feature that most improves the rep's core question, and it depends on the contacts-must-be-right work above.

4. Raw-versus-enriched export. (Easy-to-moderate; the in-app toggle already exists.) The app already flips the whole surface between enriched and raw with one control. Extend that to export: let a rep or an ops user pull either the raw scraped source data or Dan's enriched-and-verified layer as a HubSpot/Clay-ready file. This makes the dataset portable and starts it on the path to being a product in its own right.

Mid term: proactive, location-aware, and a real action loop

5. Proactive location-aware intelligence. (Harder; needs a real routing API and stored freshness.) Territory today clusters and orders. The next level is proactive: "You're in Denver. Check in with these three, they're overdue and one just went hot. Here's the optimal drive-time route." That means a real routing/directions API for true drive-time optimization (not straight-line order), plus enough stored freshness signals to know who is overdue and who is hot without a live fetch per rooftop. This turns Territory from a map you read into a plan you follow, and it is the step that makes Dan feel like it is thinking ahead of the rep.

6. Calendar and WhatsApp booking as a verifiable action loop. (Harder; external integrations plus a correctness contract.) Calendar is already read into Territory. Close the loop: let Dan (or the rep, in one tap) book the appointment, into the calendar, and confirm it over WhatsApp or SMS, with the booking landing on the same one-history timeline. The right way to build this is as a verifiable action, an environment where success is defined and checkable (the event exists, the confirmation was delivered, the slot did not collide), so the automation can be trusted to run without a human watching each one. Booking is where Dan stops being an intelligence tool and becomes an assistant that does things, and building it verifiably is what keeps that trustworthy.

7. A full life-assistant for the rep. (Incremental once 5 and 6 land.) Once Dan owns the rep's who-to-call, his route, and his bookings, it is a short step to owning the connective tissue of his day: prep notes before a meeting, a nudge when he is near an overdue account, an end-of-day recap that writes itself from the timeline. Each piece is small; the compounding is large, because every piece removes one more reason for the rep to open another app.

Longer term: the sales-intelligence vision

8. Sales intelligence that knows where you are, who you're talking to, and what to do next. (Ambitious; a synthesis, not a single feature.) The endgame is a tool that fuses location, relationship state, and live signals into a continuous recommendation: it knows the rep is standing in a specific dealership, knows the history with that rooftop, knows who just walked into the frame, and recommends the next move and the words for it, in the moment. Every near- and mid-term item is a component of this. The reason to build them in order is that this vision is only believable on top of a correct dataset, a unified contact, a real route, and a trustworthy action loop.

9. Dan as an autonomous BDR that reps supervise. (Ambitious; a governance problem as much as a technical one.) The motion engine already runs autonomously in simulation with hard safety rails. The long-term shape is Dan working the top of the funnel on his own, calls, texts, treats, at scale, while reps supervise rather than execute: they set scope and budget, review what Dan did, and step in only when a lead goes hot. The one-history timeline is the foundation for this, because supervision requires that the human and the machine share a single, auditable record of every touch. The hard part is not the automation; it is the legibility and the controls that make a human comfortable letting it run.

10. The dataset as a product in its own right. (Moderate-to-hard; a packaging and freshness problem.) The raw-plus-enriched split is not just an internal toggle; it is a product boundary. A continuously refreshed, provenance-carrying North American franchise-dealer dataset, offered raw or enriched, is valuable to anyone selling into this market, not only to Pam's reps. The trust tiers and per-value sourcing are exactly what make such a dataset defensible to a buyer. This is the step where the work done to make the internal tool honest becomes an external asset.

Risks, and how the design already mitigates each

Every one of these risks is real, and for each, the honest answer is not "it can't happen" but "here is the design that reduces the blast radius."

Data credibility. The core risk is that a rep acts on a wrong fact and loses trust in the whole tool, permanently. The mitigation is the entire provenance stance: sourced-versus-inferred-versus-live labeling, per-value sources with dates, blank-beats-wrong empty states, and validation that says "not yet verified" instead of a false "verified." The design is built so that when Dan is uncertain, it shows uncertainty rather than laundering it into false confidence. The residual work, the airtight reconciliation when sources disagree, is exactly the credibility item flagged as top priority above.

Compliance: TCPA, A2P 10DLC, AI disclosure. Autonomous calling and texting into a regulated space is the sharpest risk in the whole product. The design already carries several of the right defaults into the code: the human-initiated call lock so a real dealership is never auto-dialed, quiet-hours gating, a global kill switch, per-tick and budget caps, dry-run by default so nothing sends without an explicit flag, and a printed commitment on every record that "Dan always says he's an AI and never sells on the first call." The near-term per-enrollment consent gate is the missing piece that turns these defaults into a fully defensible compliance posture: consent logged per rooftop, AI disclosure on every automated contact, registered messaging, and honored opt-outs flowing straight to the exit guard. The point is that the guardrails live in the engine, not in a rep's discretion.

Over-automation eroding trust. If Dan automates too aggressively, reps stop believing it is on their side, and dealerships start experiencing it as spam. The design mitigates this at the level of stance, not just settings. Autonomy governs who decides, never whether the safety default holds; the status bar always tells the rep the truth about which mode he is in; the motion never sells on the first call and holds texts and treats until a rooftop shows interest; and hard exit conditions stop Dan the instant a deal goes live so a human is never talked over by a machine. The one-history timeline keeps every automated touch visible to the rep, so automation is supervised by default rather than hidden.

Dependence on third-party data. OSM, OEM locators, Google, and any paid provider can change terms, throttle, block, or degrade. The pipeline is already architected against single-source fragility: OSM is the backbone, OEM locators are additive and degrade gracefully to zero when blocked without fabricating anything, and every source is an adapter behind a common interface so a new one is a drop-in and a failing one does not take the dataset down. The trust tier is literally a count of how many independent sources agree, which means the product's credibility model is designed around not trusting any single source. That is the deepest mitigation of all: dependence is diffused by construction, and the more sources Dan reconciles, the stronger, not the more fragile, the record becomes, as long as the reconciliation stays honest.
